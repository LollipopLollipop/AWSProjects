      A Day at the MSB (with AutoScaling)

      Learning Objectives

      • Configure and Deploy an Elastic Load Balancer on AWS

      A day in the life of a Junior System Architect at the MSB

      In Project 2.2 you get to experience one day in the life of a junior MSB system architect. As a junior system architect you have an 8-hour workday, where you must ensure that the data center is responding reliably and efficiently to requests from the field agents.

      Note: For this checkpoint, assign the tag with Key: Project and Value: 2.2 for all resources

      A Note on Time Warping
      Unfortunately we do not have the budget (and most of you do not have the time) to conduct many 8 hour long tests. So, for the rest of this module, you can assume that 1 hour of time in the write-up corresponds to 5 minutes of MSB-time. Thus an 8-hour-long “work-day” is only 40 minutes of your time.

      System architects earn a salary [this corresponds to grade on the checkpoint quiz] relative to a day’s performance. The minimum requirement is that the mean requests per second for the entire 8-hours of good traffic (explained below) is over 2500 rps (this corresponds to the mean rps of the 40-minute test). On a typical day, there are two DDoS spikes that cause the average rps to go down dramatically. The “good” traffic (requests to the /track-person page of the data center VM) is fairly regular throughout the day. The bad traffic (DDoS attacks) occur in several spikes across the day. Unfortunately, system architects have a daily limit of 30 instance-hours.

      A Note on Instance-Hours
      An instance-hour is the unit of cost when one m3.medium instance is billed for a period of one hour. For the purposes of our tests, time-warping makes 1 hour of MSB time correspond to 5 minutes of real time. So, if you have two instances running for the entire 40 minute test, that corresponds to 16 instance-hours. Similarly, if you have three instances that are launched together for 8 minutes, you will be charged for 3 instances * 2 hours = 6 instance-hours.

      For each instance-hour used beyond the limit, a small amount is deducted from your salary [this corresponds to one point on the checkpoint quiz]. Thus, to maximise your earnings, the system should use the bare minimum number of instances required for most of the day, quickly scale up to handle the DDoS spikes as they occur, and then scale down as soon as the DDoS spike has ended. You can visualize your instance-hours using the “Healthy Hosts” graph in the ELB page

      There is a second type of bonus, which is for ensuring a minimum quality of service despite encountering a DDoS spike. You earn a bonus for each minute of the test that your performance is better than 1500 rps. [We do not expect you to get 1500+ rps for all 40 minutes of the test to get a full score for this question].

      You should use an ELB to divide traffic evenly between your instances. Using Auto Scaling and CloudWatch, you can scale your system to handle the traffic. In the next section, we give you an example of Auto Scaling rules, but you should feel free to devise your own rules to meet the target.

      Putting it all together

      For this part of the project (P2.2), you are required to write a program (using either the Java, boto or CLI API) to do the following set of steps. You may wish to ignore setting up CloudWatch alarms and an AutoScaling Group (Steps 2-6 could be replaced by attaching a fixed number of instances to the Elastic Load Balancer) on your first run (use only an ELB and several Data Center instances). You may also choose to use the AWS Web Console to do this for all your runs (but the last run must be automated using code). Once you analyze the traffic pattern using a fixed set of instances, you could continue with the full set of steps below:

      1. Create an ELB that:
        • Redirects HTTP:80 requests from the load balancer to HTTP:80 on the instance
        • Record the DNS Name of the created ELB
        • Set up the page /heartbeat?username=<yourandrewid> as the health check page
        • If you remember from last week, a data center instance needs to be “activated” by passing it your username. This step ensures that the ELB passes your username to your data center instances.
      2. Create a Launch Configuration for the instances that will become part of the auto scaling group, with the following parameters:
        • AMI ID: ami-ec14ba84
        • Instance Type: m3.medium
        • Detailed Monitoring: enabled
      3. After analyzing the traffic pattern, figure out a good rule for Scale Out and Scale In operations. Below we define the following default rules to give you an idea of the various parameters that need to be set. Please note that you may find it difficult to achieve a passing grade using these parameters.
        • Minimum Instance Size: 1
        • Maximum Instance Size: 15
        • Desired Instance Capacity: 1
        • ELB Name: MyELB <choose your ELB name, make sure it’s alphanumeric>
      4. Create the following Auto Scale Policies :
        • Create a ScaleOut policy that automatically adds a single instance to the auto scaling group.
        • Create a ScaleIn policy that automatically removes a single instance from the auto scaling group.
        • Link your Auto Scaling group to your ELB.
      5. Create CloudWatch Alarms that invoke the appropriate policy for the following scenarios:
        • Scale out when the group's CPU load exceeds 80% on average over a 5 minute interval.
        • Scale in when the group's CPU load is below 20% on average over a 5 minute interval.
      6. Link the CloudWatch Alarms to the ScaleOut and ScaleIn rules of your Auto Scaling Group.
      7. Create a load generator (ami-32d37a5a) instance of size m3.medium and activate it using the following URL: 
        http://<load-generator>/username?username=<your_andrew_id>
      8. Before launching the test, you may wish to warm up your load balancer using the URL: http://<load-generator-dns>/warmup Enter your ELB DNS and a testId of your choice here. In case you’ve forgotten from last week, a testId is a unique alphanumeric string that you should assign to each of your tests, so that you can view the logs later. You may choose to do this step multiple times until you are satisfied that your ELB has warmed up. The instance-hours used in the warm up phase do not count towards your final grade.
      9. Once your ELB is warmed up, start the test by visiting the URL: http://<your-load-generator-dns>/begin-phase-2

        Once again, enter your ELB DNS and a testId of your choice here. This will start a 40-minute long test. You can view the progress of the test using the URL.

        Your code should request the following HTTP URL:

        http://<your-load-generator-dns>/begin-phase-2?dns=<your-elb-dns>&testId=<your-test-id>

        You can view the progress of this test using the page:

        http:/<your-load-generator-dns>/view-logs?name=result_<yourUsername>_<yourTestId>

        The third line of the page will contain a traffic pattern token. You need to enter this token in the checkpoint quiz. It helps us identify you and the traffic pattern used for your test while grading.

      10. You can monitor your ELB using the web console:

        The graph above is for HTTP 2xx requests (successful requests). There are other graphs for failed HTTP requests (non-2xx). If you are seeing a large number of non-2xx pages, it indicates that you have misconfigured your ELB or that you are not scaling fast enough to handle the spikes.

      11. The test will give you updates of the rps for the good traffic every minute. You can see them at the URL: 
        http://<your-load-generator>/view-logs?name=result_<username>_<testId>.txt

      12. You can also view a sample of 100 requests recently received by a data center instance using the URL: http://<any-data-center-instance>/view-logs

        This page updates every 5 seconds. You will see some requests for tracking users, interspersed with DDoS traffic. As the DDoS traffic spikes, you will notice that the number of good requests handled becomes much less than the DDoS traffic. This page could give you an intuition about what is happening on your system.

      13. At the end of 40 minutes, you will be told your average rps for the entire test and given a final token to hand in. Please submit these in the OLI checkpoint quiz.
      14. Your code should terminate all resources except for the load generator and the load balancer. Once terminated, you can calculate the instance-hours either by using the CloudWatch alarms that you used to Scale Out and Scale In, or by using the “Healthy Hosts” chart on the ELB Monitoring View.

      You can launch this test several times, as long as you are within the overall project budget limits. Your performance will depend on how well you configure the Auto Scaling Group and CloudWatch alarm criteria.

      To do well this week, read (and think) about the various parameters that you can modify to achieve your performance targets. Some of the important criteria are listed here for your reference:

      ParameterAverage score for a passing grade (Passing corresponds to 75% on that question)
      Mean rps for the full 40 minute test 2000
      Number of bonuses earned (you earn one bonus for each minute of the test where you handle over 1500 rps) 20
      Number of instance-hours used 35

      You may wish to perform several runs-- one without Auto Scaling, the next with a set of Auto Scaling rules based on your observations of the static test, followed by a run with modified rules to perform well on the checkpoint. Realize that CloudWatch provides very interesting metrics that you can use to correctly identify the load on the system. Try to match the CloudWatch metrics to the number of HTTP requests received by the ELB. Finally, scaling out is tricky because the ELB may need time to warm up to new instances. Find a way to mitigate the impact of adding new instances.
